Damascus''' ({{lang-gr|Ἰωάννης ὁ Δαμασκηνός, ''Iōannēs ho Damaskēnos''}}; {{lang-la|Ioannes Damascenus}}; {{lang-ar|يوحنا الدمشقي }}, [[ALA-LC]]: ''Yūḥannā ad-Dimashqī''), also known as '''John Damascene''' and as Χρυσορρόας / ''Chrysorrhoas'' (literally "streaming with gold"&mdash;i "the golden speaker"; c 675 or 676 &ndash; 4 December 749) was a Syrian [[monk]] and [[Priesthood (Catholic Church)|priest]] Born and raised in [[Damascus]], he died at his monastery, [[Mar Saba]], near [[Jerusalem]] lt;ref>M Walsh, ed ''Butler's Lives of the Saints'' (HarperCollins Publishers: New York, 1991), p 403 lt;/ref>A [[polymath]] whose fields of interest and contribution included [[law]], [[theology]], [[philosophy]], and [[music]], he is said by some sources to have served as a Chief Administrator to the [[Islam|Muslim]] [[caliph]] of Damascus before his ordination lt;ref>Suzanne Conklin Akbari, ''Idols in the East: European representations of Islam and the Orient, 1100-1450,'' Cornell University Press, 2009 p 204</ref><ref>David Richard Thomas, ''Syrian Christians under Islam: the first thousand years,'' Brill 2001 p 19 lt;/ref> He wrote works expounding the Christian faith, and composed hymns which are still used both [[liturgy|liturgically]] in [[Eastern Christianity|Eastern Christian]] practice throughout the world as well as in western [[Lutheranism]] at Easter lt;ref>''Lutheran Service Book'' (Concordia Publishing House, St Louis, 2006), pp 478, 487 lt;/ref> He is one of the Fathers of the [[Eastern Orthodox]] church and is best known for his strong defense of icons lt;ref name=Aquilinap222>{{Harvnb|Aquilina|1999|p=222}}</ref> The [[Catholic Church]] regards him as a [[Doctor of the Church]], often referred to as the ''Doctor of the Assumption'' due to his writings on the [[Assumption of Mary]] lt;ref>{{cite book |last=Rengers |first=Christopher |title=The 33 Doctors of the Church |url=http://books oogle om/books?id=gC_bAAAACAAJ |year=2000 |publisher=Tan Books |isbn=978-0-89555-440-6 |page=200}}</ref>The most common source of information for the life of John of Damascus is a work attributed to one John of Jerusalem, identified therein as the [[Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem|Patriarch of Jerusalem]] lt;ref name=Sahas32>{{Harvnb|Sahas|1972|p=32}}</ref> This is an excerpted translation into Greek of an earlier Arabic text The Arabic original contains a prologue not found in most other translations, and was written by an Arab monk, Michael Michael explained that he decided to write his biography in 1084 because none was available in his day However, the main Arabic text seems to have been written by an earlier author sometime between the early 9th and late 10th centuries AD lt;ref name=Sahas32/> Written from a hagiographical point of view and prone to exaggeration and some legendary details, it is not the best historical source for his life, but is widely reproduced and considered to contain elements of some value lt;ref name=Sahas35>{{Harvnb|Sahas|1972|p=35}}</ref> The hagiographic novel ''[[Barlaam and Josaphat]]'', traditionally attributed to John, is in fact a work of the 10th century lt;ref>R Volk, ed ''Historiae animae utilis de Barlaam et Ioasaph'' (Berlin, 2006) lt;/ref>==Family background==John was born into a prominent Arab family known as Mansour in [[Damascus]] in the 7th century AD lt;ref name="Brownp307">{{harvnb|Brown|2003|p=307}}</ref><ref name="McEnhillNewlandsp154">{{harvnb|McEnhill|Newlands|2004|p=154}}</ref> His grandfather had been responsible for the taxes of the region under the Emperor [[Heraclius]] and seems to have played a role in the capitulation of Damascus to the troops of Khalid b al-Walid in 635 lt;ref name="Brownp307"/> [[Patriarch Eutychius of Alexandria|Eutychius]], a 10th-century [[Melkite]] patriarch mentions him as one high ranking official involved in the surrender of the city to the Muslims lt;ref name=Sahas17>{{Harvnb|Sahas|1972|p=17}}</ref> When the region came under Arab [[Muslim]] occupation in the late 7th century AD, the court at Damascus retained its large complement of Christian civil servants, John's grandfather among them lt;ref name="Brownp307"/><ref name=Sahas17/> John's father, Sarjun (Sergius) or Ibn Mansur, went on to serve the [[Umayyad]] [[caliph]]s lt;ref name="Brownp307"/> According to John of Jerusalem and some later versions of his life, after his father's death John also served as an official to the caliphal court before leaving to become a monk This claim, that John actually served in a Muslim court, has been questioned since he is never mentioned in Muslim sources, which however do refer to his father Sarjun (Sergius) as a secretary in the caliphal administration lt;ref>{{harvnb|Hoyland|1996|p=481}}</ref> In addition, John's own writings never refer to any experience in a Muslim court It is believed that John became a monk at [[Mar Saba]], and that he was ordained as a priest in 735 lt;ref name="Brownp307"/><ref name="McEnhillNewlandsp154"/>==Education==One of the ''vitae'' describes his father's desire for him to "learn not only the books of the Muslims, but those of the Greeks as well quot; From this it has been suggested that John may have grown up bilingual lt;ref>Valantasis, p 455</ref> John does indeed show some knowledge of the [[Quran]], which he criticizes harshly lt;ref>{{harvnb|Hoyland|1996|pp=487–489}}</ref>Other sources describes his education in Damascus as having been conducted in accordance with the principles of [[Paideia|Hellenic education]], termed "secular" by one source and "Classical Christian" by another lt;ref name="Louthp284">{{harvnb|Louth|2002|p=284}}</ref><ref name="Butlerp36">{{harvnb|Butler|Jones|Burns|2000|p=36}}</ref> One account identifies his tutor as a monk by the name of Cosmas, who had been kidnapped by Arabs from his home in [[Sicily]], and for whom John's father paid a great price Under the instruction of Cosmas, who also taught John's orphan friend (the future St [[Cosmas of Maiuma]]), John is said to have made great advances in music, [[astronomy]] and theology, soon rivalling [[Pythagoras]] in arithmetic and [[Euclid]] in [[geometry]] lt;ref name="Butlerp36"/> As a refugee from Italy, Cosmas brought with him the scholarly traditions of [[Western Christianity]] =Career ==John had at least one and possibly two careers: one (less well-documented) as a civil servant for the [[Umayyad Caliphate|Caliph]] in Damascus, and the other (better-attested) as a priest and monk at the [[Mar Saba]] monastery near Jerusalem One source believes John left Damascus to become a monk around 706, when [[al-Walid I]] increased the Islamicisation of the Caliphate's administration lt;ref name=Louthp9>{{Harvnb|Louth|2003|p=9}}</ref> However, Muslim sources only mention that his father Sarjun (Sergius) left the administration around this time, and fail to name John at all lt;ref>{{harvnb|Hoyland|1996|pp=481}}</ref> During the next two decades, culminating in the [[Siege of Constantinople (717-718)]], the [[Umayyad Caliphate]] progressively occupied the borderlands of the Byzantine Empire An editor of John's works, Father [[Michel Le Quien|Le Quien]], has shown that John was already a monk at Mar Saba before the dispute over iconoclasm, explained below lt;ref name=catholic/>In the early 8th century AD, [[Iconoclasm (Byzantine)|iconoclasm]], a movement opposed to the veneration of icons, gained acceptance in the [[Byzantine]] court In 726, despite the protests of [[Patriarch Germanus I of Constantinople|St Germanus]], [[List of Ecumenical Patriarchs of Constantinople|Patriarch of Constantinople]], Emperor [[Leo III the Isaurian|Leo III]] (who had forced the emperor to abdicate and himself assumed the throne in 717 immediately before the great siege) issued his first edict against the [[veneration]] of images and their exhibition in public places lt;ref name=cathen>{{cite web|last=O'Connor|first=John Bonaventure|title=St John Damascene|url=http://www ewadvent rg/cathen/08459b tm|work=The Catholic Encyclopedia|publisher=New Advent|accessdate=9 April 2011}}</ref> All agree that John of Damascus undertook a spirited defence of holy images in three separate publications The earliest of these works, his ''"Apologetic Treatises against those Decrying the Holy Images"'', secured his reputation He not only attacked the Byzantine emperor, but adopted a simplified style that allowed the controversy to be followed by the common people, stirring rebellion among those of [[Christianity|Christian]] faith Decades after his death, John's writings would play an important role during the [[Second Council of Nicaea]] (787), which convened to settle the icon dispute {citation needed|date=December 2014}}John's biography recounts at least one episode deemed improbable or legendary lt;ref name=catholic/><ref name="Jamesonp24">{{harvnb|Jameson|2008|p=24}}</ref> Leo III reportedly sent forged documents to the caliph which implicated John in a plot to attack [[Damascus]] The caliph then ordered John's right hand be cut off and hung up in public view Some days afterwards, John asked for the restitution of his hand, and prayed fervently to the [[Theotokos]] before her icon: thereupon, his hand is said to have been miraculously restored lt;ref name=catholic>{{cite web|url=http://www atholic rg/saints/saint hp?saint_id=66|title=St John of Damascus|author=Catholic Online|work=catholic rg}}</ref> In gratitude for this miraculous healing, he attached a silver hand to the icon, which thereafter became known as the "Three-handed", or [[Trojeručica|Tricheirousa]] lt;ref>{{harvnb|Louth|2002|pp=17, 19}}</ref>==Last days==John died in 749 as a revered Father of the Church, and is recognized as a [[saint]] He is sometimes called the last of the [[Church Fathers]]
