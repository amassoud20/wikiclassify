historical record that van Eyck was considered a revolutionary master across northern Europe within his lifetime; his designs and methods were heavily copied and reproduced His motto, one of the first and still most distinctive signatures in art history, ''<small>ALS IK KAN</small>'' ("AS I CAN"), a pun on his name,<ref>Nash (2008), 152</ref> first appeared in 1433 on ''[[Portrait of a Man in a ''[[Madonna of Chancellor Rolin]]'', ''[[Lucca Madonna]]'' and ''[[Virgin and Child with Canon van der Paele]]'' He married the much younger [[Portrait of Margaret van Eyck|Margaret]] probably around 1432, about the same time he bought a house in Bruges; she is unmentioned before he relocated, while the first of their two children was born in 1434 Very little is known of Margaret, even her maiden name is lost - contemporary records refer to her mainly as ''Damoiselle Marguierite'' lt;ref name="t149"/> She is thought to have been of aristocratic birth, though from the lower nobility, evidenced from her clothes in this portrait which are fashion but not of the sumptuousness worn by the bride in the ''[[Arnolfini Portrait]]'' Later, as the widow of a renowned painter Margaret was afforded a modest pension by the city of Bruges after Jan's death At least some of this income was invested in [[lottery]] lt;ref>Van Der Elst (2005), 65</ref>Records from 1437 on suggest that he was held in high esteem by the upper ranks of Burgundian nobility while also accepting many foreign commissions He died young in July 1441, leaving behind many unfinished works to be completed by workshop characterised by the use of symbolism and biblical references lt;ref name="w11">Ward (1994), 11</ref> Van Eyck incorporated a wide variety of iconographic elements, often conveying what he saw as a co-existence of the spiritual and material worlds The iconography was embedded in the work unobtrusively; typically the references comprised small but key background details lt;ref name="w11" /> Van Eyck pioneered, and his innovations were taken up and developed by van der Weyden, Memling and Christus Each employed rich and complex iconographical elements to create a heightened sense of contemporary beliefs and spiritual ideals lt;ref name="Harbison601"/>[[File:La Madone au Chanoine Van der Paele pg|300px|left|thumb|''[[Virgin and Child with Canon van der Paele]]'', c 1434–36 [[Groeningemuseum]], [[Bruges]] Van Eyck's Marian paintings are suffused with iconographic detail]]Craig Harbison describes the blending of realism and symbolism as perhaps "the most important aspect of early Flemish art" lt;ref name ="Harbison601">Harbison (1984), 601</ref> The embedded symbols were meant to meld into the scenes and "was a deliberate strategy to create an experience of spiritual revelation quot;<ref name="Ward9">Ward (1994), 9</ref> Van Eyck's religious paintings in particular "always present the spectator with a transfigured view of visible reality" lt;ref name="Harbison589"/> To him the day-to-day is harmoniously steeped in symbolism, such that, according to Harbison, "descriptive data were rearranged&nbsp; so that they illustrated not earthly existence but what he considered supernatural truth quot;<ref name="Harbison589">Harbison (1984), 589</ref> This blend of the earthly and heavenly evidences van Eyck's belief that the "essential truth of Christian doctrine" can be found in "the marriage of secular and sacred worlds, of reality and symbol" lt;ref name="H590">Harbison (1984), 590</ref> He depicts overly large Madonnas, whose unrealistic size shows the separation between the heavenly from earthly, but placed them in everyday settings such as churches, domestic chambers or seated with court officials lt;ref name="H590"/>Yet the earthly churches are heavily decorated with heavenly symbols A heavenly throne is clearly represented in some domestic chambers (for example in the ''[[Lucca Madonna]]'') More difficult to discern are the settings for paintings such as ''Madonna of Chancellor Rolin'', where the location is a fusion of the earthly and celestial lt;ref>Harbison (1984), 590–592</ref> Van Eyck's iconography is often so densely and intricately layered that a work has to be viewed multiple times before even the most obvious meaning of an element is apparent The symbols were often subtly woven into the paintings so that they only became apparent after close and repeated viewing,<ref name="w11" /> while much of the iconography reflects the idea that, according to John Ward, there is a "promised passage from sin and death to salvation and rebirth" lt;ref>Ward (1994), 26</ref>===Signature===[[File:Jan van Eyck - Portrait of Giovanni Arnolfini and his Wife (detail) - WGA7695 pg|thumb|300px|Detail, ''[[Arnolfini Portrait]]'', 1434]]Van Eyck was the only 15th-century Netherlandish painter to sign his canvases lt;ref name="h31">Harbison (1997), 31</ref> His signature usually contains his personal motto ''<small>ALS IK KAN</small>'' (or a variant) – "As I Can", or "As Best I Can" – taken from the Flemish saying "As I can, not as I would", and which forms a pun on his name The signature is sometimes inscribed using Greek lettering such as ''<small>AAE IXH XAN</small>'' lt;ref>Harbison (1997), 163</ref> The word ''Kan'' derives from the [[Middle Dutch]] word ''kunnen'' related to the German ''Kunst'' ("art") lt;ref name="k107" />The words may be related to a type of formula of modesty sometimes seen in medieval literature, where the writer prefaces his work with an apology for a lack of perfection,<ref name="k107">Koerner (1996), 107</ref> although, given the typical lavishness of the signatures and mottos, it may merely be a playful reference Indeed, his motto is sometimes recorded in a manner intended to mimic Christ's monogram ''<small>IHC XPC</small>'', for example in his 1440 ''[[Portrait of Christ (Vera Icon)|Portrait of Christ]]'' lt;ref name="k107" /> Further, as the signature is often a variant of "Jan van Eyck was here", it can be seen as a, perhaps somewhat arrogant, assertion of the faithfulness and trustworthiness of the record; by none other than Jan van Eyck lt;ref name="c32"/>[[File:Léal Souvenir Inscriptions pg|thumb|300px|left|Detail of ''[[Léal Souvenir]]'' showing three levels of painted inscription]]The habit of signing his work ensured that his reputation survived, and attribution has not been as difficult and uncertain as with other first generation artists of the early Netherlandish school lt;ref>Macfall (2004), 17</ref> The signatures are usually completed in a decorative script, often of a kind reserved for legal documents, as can be seen in ''[[Léal Souvenir]]'' and the ''[[Arnolfini Portrait]]'',<ref name="c200">Campbell (1998), 200</ref> the latter of which is signed "Johannes de eyck fuit hic 1434" ("Jan van Eyck was here 1434"), a way of recording his presence he ''[[Dresden Triptych]]'' is van
