manifestation of a struggle taking place between poorer citizens and the aristocrats?===Moral reform===In his poems, Solon portrays Athens as being under threat from the unrestrained greed and arrogance of its citizens lt;ref>Demosthenes 19 (''On the Embassy'') 254–5</ref> Even the earth ([[Gaia (mythology)|Gaia]]), the mighty mother of the gods, had been enslaved lt;ref>''Athenaion Politeia'' 12 (quoting Solon) [[s:Athenian Constitution#12]]</ref> The visible symbol of this perversion of the natural and social order was a boundary marker called a ''horos'', a wooden or stone pillar indicating that a farmer was in debt or under contractual obligation to someone else, either a noble patron or a creditor lt;ref>Stanton, G R ''Athenian Politics c800–500BC: A Sourcebook'', Routledge, London (1991), pages 55–6 notes 3 and 4</ref> Up until Solon's time, land was the inalienable property of a family or clan<ref>[[Harold Innis|Innis H ] ''Empire and Communications'' (Rowman and Littlefield, 2007) pages 91–92</ref> and it could not be sold or mortgaged This was no disadvantage to a clan with large landholdings since it could always rent out farms in a [[sharecropping]] system A family struggling on a small farm however could not use the farm as security for a loan even if it owned the farm Instead the farmer would have to offer himself and his family as security, providing some form of slave labour in lieu of repayment Equally, a family might voluntarily pledge part of its farm income or labour to a powerful clan in return for its protection Farmers subject to these sorts of arrangements were loosely known as ''hektemoroi''<ref>Stanton, G R ''Athenian Politics c800–500BC: A Sourcebook'', Routledge, London (1991), page 38 note 3</ref> indicating that they either paid or kept a sixth of a farm's annual yield lt;ref>Stanton, G R ''Athenian Politics c800–500BC: A Sourcebook'', Routledge, London (1990), page 35 note 3</ref><ref>Kirk G, Historia Vol 26 (1977) 369–370</ref><ref>Woodhouse W ''Solon the Liberator: A Study of the Agrarian Problem in Attika in the Seventh Century'' (Oxford University Press 1938)</ref> In the event of 'bankruptcy', or failure to honour the contract stipulated by the ''horoi'', farmers and their families could in fact be sold into slavery [File:Amphora olive-gathering BM B226 pg|thumb|right|250px|This 6th Century Athenian black-figure urn, in the [[British Museum]], depicts the olive harvest Many farmers, enslaved for debt, would have worked on large estates for their creditors ]Solon's reform of these injustices was later known and celebrated among Athenians as the [[Seisachtheia]] (shaking off of burdens) lt;ref name="autogenerated2">Athenaion Politeia 6 [[s:Athenian Constitution#6]]</ref><ref name="autogenerated3">Plutarch ''Solon'' 15 [[s:Lives (Dryden translation)/Solon#15]]</ref> As with all his reforms, there is considerable scholarly debate about its real significance Many scholars are content to accept the account given by the ancient sources, interpreting it as a cancellation of debts, while others interpret it as the abolition of a type of feudal relationship, and some prefer to explore new possibilities for interpretation lt;ref name="E Harris, 1997"/> The reforms included:*annulment of all contracts symbolised by the ''horoi'' lt;ref name="autogenerated4">Athenaion Politeia 12 , ''quoting Solon'' [[s:Athenian Constitution#12]]</ref>*prohibition on a debtor's person being used as security for a loan lt;ref name="autogenerated2"/><ref name="autogenerated3"/>*release of all Athenians who had been enslaved lt;ref name="autogenerated4"/>The removal of the ''horoi'' clearly provided immediate economic relief for the most oppressed group in Attica, and it also brought an immediate end to the enslavement of Athenians by their countrymen Some Athenians had already been sold into slavery abroad and some had fled abroad to escape enslavement – Solon proudly records in verse the return of this diaspora lt;ref>''Solon quoted in'' Athenaion Politeia 12 [[s:Athenian Constitution#12]]</ref> It has been cynically observed, however, that few of these unfortunates were likely to have been recovered lt;ref>Forrest G ''The Oxford History of the Classical World'' ed Griffin J and Murray O (Oxford University Press, 1995) page 32</ref> It has been observed also that the ''seisachtheia'' not only removed slavery and accumulated debt, it also removed the ordinary farmer's only means of obtaining further credit lt;ref>Stanton, G R ''Athenian Politics c800–500BC; a Sourcebook'' Routledge, London (1991) page 57 note 1</ref>The seisachtheia however was merely one set of reforms within a broader agenda of moral reformation Other reforms included:*the abolition of extravagant dowries lt;ref>Plutarch ''Solon'' 20 [[s:Lives (Dryden translation)/Solon#20]]</ref>*legislation against abuses within the system of inheritance, specifically with relation to the ''[[epikleros]]'' (i a female who had no brothers to inherit her father's property and who was traditionally required to marry her nearest paternal relative in order to produce an heir to her father's estate) lt;ref name=Grant49>Grant, Michael ''The Rise of the Greeks'' New York: Charles Scribner's Sons, 1988 p 49</ref>*entitlement of any citizen to take legal action on behalf of another lt;ref>Athenaion Politeia 9 [[s:Athenian Constitution#9]]</ref><ref>Plutarch ''Solon'' 18 [[s:Lives (Dryden translation)/Solon#18]]</ref>*the disenfranchisement of any citizen who might refuse to take up arms in times of civil strife, a measure that was intended to counteract dangerous levels of political apathy lt;ref>Athenaion Politeia 8 [[s:Athenian Constitution#8]]</ref><ref>Stanton, G R ''Athenian Politics c800–500BC; a Sourcebook'' Routledge, London (1991) page 72 note 17</ref><ref>Plutarch ''Solon'' 20 [[s:Lives (Dryden translation)/Solon#20]]</ref><ref>Goldstein J Historia Vol 21 (1972) 538–545 lt;/ref><ref>Develin R Historia Vol 26 (1977) 507–508 lt;/ref>[[Demosthenes]] claimed that the city's subsequent golden age included "personal modesty and frugality" among the Athenian aristocracy lt;ref>[http://www literature et/Demosthenes/Oration_on_the_Regulation_of_the_State/ Demosthenes ''On Organization'']</ref> Perhap Solon, by both personal example and legislated reform, established a precedent for this decorum A heroic sense of civic duty later united Athenians against the might of the [[Achaemenid Empire|Persians]] Perhaps this public spirit was instilled in them by Solon and his reforms Also see [[#Solon and Athenian sexuality|Solon and Athenian sexuality]] ==Aftermath of Solon's reforms===After completing his work of reform, Solon surrendered his extraordinary authority and left the country According to [[Herodotus]]<ref>Herodotus 1 9 (e Campbell's translation [[:gutenberg:2707|2707]])</ref> the country was bound by Solon to maintain his reforms for 10 years, whereas according to [[Plutarch]]<ref name="ReferenceA"/> and the author of the ''Athenian c800–55BC; a Sourcebook'' Routledge, London (1991) page 84</ref> considers the time-span given by Herodotus to be historically accurate because it fits the 10 years that Solon was said to have been absent from the country lt;ref>Plutarch ''Solon'' 25 [[s:Lives (Dryden translation)/Solon#25]]</ref> Within 4 years of Solon's departure, the old social rifts re-appeared, but with some new even been said that some people blamed Solon for their troubles lt;ref>Athenaion Politeia 13 [[s:Athenian Constitution#13]]</ref> Eventually one of Solon's relatives, [[Peisistratos]], ended the factionalism by force, thus instituting an unconstitutionally gained [[Tyrant|tyranny]] In Plutarch's account, Solon accused Athenians of stupidity and cowardice for allowing this to happen
