output was vocal His early works in that sphere include cantatas written for his unsuccessful attempts at the Prix de Rome His other vocal music from that period shows Debussy's influence, in what voice,<ref>Nichols (2011), p 280</ref> and he seems to have preferred his best-known cycle, ''Shéhérazade'', to be sung by a woman, although a tenor voice is a permitted alternative in the score valse'' and the two concertos All the other orchestral works were written either for the stage, as in ''Daphnis et Chloé'', or as a reworking of piano pieces, ''Alborada del gracioso'' and ''Une lt;ref>Sackville-West and Shawe-Taylor, p 611</ref>In some of his scores from the 1920s, including ''Daphnis et Chloé'', Ravel frequently divides his upper strings, having them play in six to eight the 1930s he tended to simplify his orchestral textures The lighter tone of the G major Piano Concerto follows the models of [[Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart|Mozart]] and Saint-Saëns, alongside use of jazz-like themes lt;ref>Orenstein (1991), pp 204–205</ref> The critics [[Edward Sackville-West]] and [[Desmond Shawe-Taylor (music critic)|Desmond Shawe-Taylor]] comment that in the slow movement, "one of the most beautiful tunes Ravel ever invented", the composer "can truly be said to join hands with Mozart" lt;ref>Sackville-West and Shawe-Taylor, p 610</ref> The most popular of Ravel's orchestral works, ''Boléro'' (1928), was conceived several years before its completion; in 1924 he said that he was contemplating "a symphonic poem without a subject, where the whole interest will be in the rhythm" lt;ref>Nichols (2011), p 302</ref>Ravel made orchestral versions of piano works by Schumann, Chabrier, Debussy and Mussorgsky's piano suite ''Pictures at an Exhibition'' Orchestral
