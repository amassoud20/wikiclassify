of Fame began On May 9, 2011, the City of Lubbock held a ribbon-cutting ceremony for the Buddy and Maria Elena Holly Plaza, the new home of the statue and the Walk of Fame {sfn|Kerns, William|2011}} The same year, a star bearing Holly's name was placed on the [[Hollywood Walk of Fame]], commemorating his 75th birthday {sfn|Duke, Alan|2011}}===Influence===Teenagers [[John Lennon]] and [[Paul McCartney]] saw Holly for the first time when he appeared on ''[[Sunday Night at the London Palladium]]'' {sfn|Humphries, Patrick|p=73|2003}} The two had recently met and begun their musical association They studied Holly's records, learned his performance style and lyricism, and based their act around his persona Inspired by Holly's insect-themed Crickets, they chose to name their band "[[The Beatles|Beatles]]" Lennon and McCartney later cited Holly as their main influence {sfn|Riley, Tim|p=67-70|2011}}Lennon's band [[the Quarrymen]] covered "That'll Be the Day" during their first recording session in 1958 {sfn|Gaar, Gillian|p=238|2013}} Years later, during breaks in the Beatles' first appearance on ''The Ed Sullivan Show'' on February 9, 1964, [[CBS]] coordinator Vic Calandra talked with McCartney and Lennon Lennon asked him about Holly's performances; Calandra said the musicians repeatedly expressed their appreciation of Holly {sfn|Harris, Michael|p=192-193|2014}} The Beatles recorded a close cover of Holly's version of "[[Words of Love]]", which was released on their 1964 album ''[[Beatles for Sale]]'' (in the U in June 1965 on ''[[Beatles VI]]'') During the January 1969 recording sessions for their album ''[[Let It Be]]'', the Beatles played a slow, impromptu version of "Mailman, Bring Me No More Blues" – which Holly popularized but did not write – with Lennon mimicking Holly's vocal style {sfn|Margotin, Phillipe; Guesdon, Jean-Michael|2014|p=186}} Lennon recorded a cover version of "Peggy Sue" on his 1975 album ''[[Rock 'n' Roll (John Lennon album)|Rock 'n' Roll]]'' {sfn|Blaney, John|p=163|2005}} McCartney owns the [[Music publisher (popular music)|publishing rights]] to Holly's song catalog {sfn|BBC News staff|2003}}Two nights before Holly's death, 17-year-old [[Bob Dylan]] attended the January 31, 1959, show Dylan referred to this in his 1998 Grammy acceptance speech for his ''[[Time Out of Mind]]'' being named [[Grammy Award for Album of the Year|Album of the Year]]; Dylan said, "&nbsp; amp;nbsp;when I was sixteen or seventeen years old, I went to see Buddy Holly play at [[Duluth National Guard Armory]] and I was three feet away from him&nbsp; amp;nbsp;and he looked at me And I just have some sort of feeling that he was&nbsp; amp;nbsp;with us all the time we were making this record in some kind of way" {sfn|Shelton, Robert|p=37|2011}}[[Mick Jagger]] saw Holly performing live in [[Woolwich]], London, during tour of England; Jagger particularly remembered Holly's performance of "Not Fade Away" – a song that also inspired for [[Keith Richards]], who modeled his early guitar playing on the track The Rolling Stones had a hit version of the track in 1964 {sfn|Norman, Philip|p=12|2011}} Richards later said, "[Holly] passed it on via the Beatles and via [the Rolling Stones]&nbsp; amp;nbsp;He's in everybody" {sfn|Amburn, Ellis|2014|p=274}}[[Don McLean]]'s popular 1971 [[ballad (music)|ballad]] "[[American Pie (song)|American Pie]]" was inspired by Holly's death and the day of the plane crash The song's lyric, which calls the incident "The Day the Music Died", became popularly associated with the crash McLean's album ''[[American Pie (album)|American Pie]]'' is dedicated to Holly {sfn|Crouse, Richard|p=86|2012}} In 2015, McLean wrote, "Buddy Holly would have the same stature musically whether he would have lived or died, because of his accomplishments&nbsp; amp;nbsp;By the time he was 22 years old, he had recorded some 50 tracks, most of which he had written himself&nbsp; amp;nbsp;in my view and the view of many others, a hit&nbsp; amp;nbsp;Buddy Holly and the Crickets were the template for all the rock bands that followed" {sfn|McLean, Don|2015}}[[Elton John]] was musically influenced by Holly At age thirteen, although he did not require them, John started wearing horn-rimmed glasses to imitate Holly After eight months, the glasses diminished his vision and John had to start to wear prescription lenses on a regular basis {sfn|Goldrosen, John|1979|p=8}} [[The Clash]] were also influenced by Holly, and referenced him in their song "Corner Soul" from the ''[[Sandinista!]]'' album {sfn|Fletcher, Tony|p=174|2012}} ''The Chirping Crickets'' was the first album [[Eric Clapton]] ever bought; he later saw Holly on ''Sunday Night at the London Palladium'' In his autobiography, Clapton recounted the first time he saw Holly and his Fender, saying, "I thought I'd died and gone to heaven&nbsp; amp;nbsp;it was like seeing an instrument from outer space and I said to myself: 'That's the future – that's what I want'" {sfn|Clapton, Eric|p=19|2010}}The launch of [[Bobby Vee]]'s successful musical career resulted from Holly's death; Vee was selected to replace Holly on the tour that continued after the plane crash Holly's profound influence on Vee's singing style can be heard in the songs "[[Rubber Ball]]" – the B-side of which was a cover of Holly's "Everyday" – and "Run to Him quot;{{sfn|Dean, Maury|2003|p=73}} The name of the British rock band [[the Hollies]] is often claimed as a tribute to Holly; according to the band, they admired Holly, but their name was mainly inspired by sprigs of holly in evidence around Christmas 1962 {sfn|Eder, Bruce|1996}} In an August 24, 1978, interview with ''Rolling Stone'', [[Bruce Springsteen]] told Dave Marsh, "I play Buddy Holly every night before I go on; that keeps me honest" {sfn|Deardorff II, Donald|p=16|2013}} The [[Grateful Dead]] performed the song "Not Fade Away" in concerts {sfn|Meriwether,
