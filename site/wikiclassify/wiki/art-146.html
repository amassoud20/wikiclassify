{{convert|45|km|mi}} from [[Ulm]], in the [[Kingdom of Württemberg]], then part of the [[German Empire]] He was the second of four children of Erwin Rommel Senior (1860–1913), a teacher and school brothers, one of whom died in infancy {sfn|Fraser|1993|p=8}}{{sfn|Butler|2015|pp=26–27}}At the age of 14, Rommel and a friend built a full-scale glider and were able to fly it short distances He later purchased a motorcycle, and upon getting home immediately set about taking it apart and putting it back together {sfn|Lewin|1998|p=219}} Rommel considered becoming an [[aeronautical engineer]], but at age 18 he acceded to his father's wishes and joined the local 124th Württemberg Infantry Regiment as a ''[[Fähnrich]]'' ([[Ensign (rank)|ensign]]), in 1910, studying at the Officer Cadet School in [[Danzig]] {sfn|Hoffmann|2004|p=10}} He graduated in November 1911 and was commissioned as a [[lieutenant]] in January 1912 and was assigned to the 124th Infantry in [[Weingarten, Württemberg|Weingarten]] {sfn|Butler|2015|pp=30–31}} He was posted to Ulm in March 1914 to command the No Battery, 46th Field Artillery Regiment, [[XIII (Royal Württemberg) Corps]] Until the start of Frankreich, Argonnerwald, Vorgehen von Infanterie pg|thumb|right|German infantry advance up a slope in the Argonne forest, 1915 ]During World War I, Rommel fought in [[France]] as well as in the [[Romanian Campaign (World War I)|Romanian]] and [[Italian Campaign (World War I)|Italian Campaigns]] He gained success leading small groups of men, using tactics such as infiltrating through enemy lines under cover of darkness, moving forward rapidly to a flanking position to arrive at their rear, and attacking defenders using the element of surprise {sfn|Lewin|1998|p=4}} Arriving at the front near [[Verdun]] on 22 August 1914, Rommel initially commanded a platoon in 2nd Battalion, 124th Regiment {sfn|Fraser|1993|p=25}} They were assigned to reconnaissance and courier tasks His first combat experience was on 22 August 1914, when – catching the French garrison unprepared at the village of Bleid – he and three men engaged the enemy without waiting for the rest of their platoon to arrive {sfn|Fraser|1993|pp=27–29}} Rommel was often ill while on active duty, particularly with stomach troubles and exhaustion, a problem that manifested itself from the beginning of his career {sfn|Douglas-Home|1973|p=25}} He was appointed Battalion Adjutant in September The armies continued to skirmish in open engagements throughout September, as the static [[trench warfare]] typical of the First World War was still in the future {sfn|Fraser|1993|p=31}} On 24 September Rommel was shot in the leg when he engaged several French soldiers armed only with his bayonet (he had run out of ammunition) For this action, he was awarded the [[Iron Cross]], Second Class {sfn|Fraser|1993|p=36}}On his return in January 1915, Rommel was assigned to command 9th Company, 124th Regiment, stationed in the trenches near [[Forest of Argonne|Argonne]] {sfn|Douglas-Home|1973|p=25}}{{sfn|Fraser|1993|p=39}} On 29 January, he and his platoon crawled through {{convert|100|yd}} of barbed wire to engage the French, who were positioned in blockhouses and earthworks His company was running low on ammunition and were ordered to retreat Rommel ordered an attack on one of the blockhouses to keep the enemy from opening fire on the withdrawing men For his work that day, he was awarded the Iron Cross, First Class {sfn|Fraser|1993|p=43}}He continued to soldier in the trenches of France for another nine months, and received a minor shrapnel wound to the leg on 29 June 1915 {sfn|Fraser|1993|p=43}}{{sfn|Butler|2015|p=50}} He was promoted to ''[[Oberleutnant]]'' (first lieutenant) and transferred to the newly created ''Königliche Wurttemberg Gebirgsbataillon'' (Royal Wurttemberg Mountain Battalion) of the ''[[Alpenkorps (German Empire)|Alpenkorps]]'' in September He was commander of 2nd Company, which trained in mountain warfare in Austria until December, when they were posted on a {{convert|6|mile|adj=on}} stretch of front in the [[Vosges]] mountains of [[Alsace]] {sfn|Fraser|1993|pp=43, 45}} They remained there, seeing action in reconnaissance work and raids on enemy positions until October 1916, when they were moved to the [[Southern Carpathians]] to fight the Romanians, who had joined the conflict in August {sfn|Fraser|1993|pp=48–49}} In August 1917, his unit was involved in the battle for Mount Cosna, a heavily fortified objective on the border between Hungary and Romania They succeeded after nearly two weeks of difficult uphill fighting and were withdrawn to reserve on 25 August {sfn|Fraser|1993|pp=53–60}} The unit spent six weeks recuperating in [[Carinthia]], and Rommel received leave to return to Danzig to see his wife and recover from a gunshot wound to the arm that he had received in the fight at Mount Cosna {sfn|Butler|2015|p=65}}The Mountain Battalion was next assigned to fight on the [[Italian Front (World War I)|Isonzo front]], a mountainous area which had been the scene of near-constant fighting since the entry of Italy into the war on the Allied side on 23 May 1915 The offensive known as the Twelfth Battle of the Isonzo, or the [[Battle of Caporetto]], began on 24 October 1917 with a four-hour artillery barrage {sfn|Butler|2015|pp=65–67}} Rommel's ''[[Abteilung]]'', consisting of three rifle companies and a machine gun unit, was part of an attempt to take enemy positions on three mountains: [[Kolovrat (mountain ridge)|Kolovrat]], [[Matajur]], and Stol {sfn|Hoffmann|2004|p=14}} Beginning at dawn on 25 October, Rommel took advantage of the terrain to outflank the Italians and gain control of the ridge on Kolovrat, taking 1,500 prisoners in the first three hours {sfn|Butler|2015|p=71}} Noticing that there was no field of fire on a supply road leading down to the village of Luico (now [[Livek]]), Rommel and 150 of his men proceeded down and captured the town, {{convert|2|miles}} behind enemy lines Believing the presence of Rommels' group to be proof that their lines had collapsed, a column of Italian light infantry, 2,000 strong, surrendered after a brief firefight {sfn|Butler|2015|pp=72–73}} Before dawn on the 26th, Rommel led his ''Abteilung'', now reinforced with two additional machine gun companies, toward Matajur They took the village of Jevszek without a fight, capturing another 1,600 men In spite of orders not to attack, they assaulted Matajur from an unexpected direction from behind the Italian lines, arriving at the summit shortly before noon on 27 October {sfn|Butler|2015|pp=74–77}} In two and a half days, he and his small contingent of men had captured 81 guns and 9,000 men (including 150 officers), at the loss of six dead and 30 wounded {sfn|Hoffmann|2004|p=15}} Acting as advance guard in the capture of [[Longarone]] on 9 November, he again decided to attack with a much smaller force Reinforcements continued to arrive, and fighting continued through the night Convinced that they were surrounded by an entire German division, the 1st Italian Infantry Division – 10,000 men – surrendered to Rommel at dawn For this and his work at Matajur, he (and his battalion commander, Major {{illm|Theodor Sproesser|de}}) received the order of [[Pour le Mérite]] After a week on leave in January 1918, Rommel was promoted to ''[[Hauptmann]]'' (captain) and assigned to a staff position with XLIV Army Corps, where he served for the remainder of the war {{sfn|Butler|2015|pp=78–81}}==
